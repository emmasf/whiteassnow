When you think of Snow White, you probably picture the film that launched the Disney empire. The tale was actually first published by Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm in their 1812 book, Kinder-und Hausmaerchen, or Tales of Children and the Home.

The Grimms were not writing for children. They were academic linguists, interested in preserving German language and culture through its oral traditions. The brothers hoped to record traditional German values along with the old German language and strengthen the German presence in an era of growing national identity throughout Western Europe.

They may have been right to think that Germany’s values lay in its stories, but these tales are not uniquely German. Versions of the story appear all over the globe: Scotland, Ireland, France, Spain, Portugal, Italy, Hungary, Romania, Albania, Greece, Morocco, Algeria, Libya, West Africa, Gabon, Angola and Mozambique, to name a few. The details vary, but the basic elements of the tale—young beauty, a jealous mother figure, exile, magical poisoning, and a long sleep—are universal. Perhaps the German values that the Grimms were trying to capture were universal as well.
